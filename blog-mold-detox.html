How to Detox from Mold: What Actually Worked for My Family

In 2020 we discovered our home was full of mold — and that all of us had severe, test-confirmed mold toxicity. What followed was the hardest reset of our lives: throwing away almost everything we owned, and six months detoxing on a beach in Ecuador. This is the honest, practical version of what mold detox actually takes.

Christina CarterChronic Lyme & Mold Advocate · Updated July 2026 · 14 min read

Last reviewed July 6, 2026

Most "how to detox from mold" articles are written by people who've never lived it. I have. So before I give you the practical steps, let me tell you where they come from — because the strategy only makes sense once you understand the mess we were digging out of.

Our mold story

In 2020, during lockdown, we discovered our house had mold — and testing confirmed that our entire family had severe mold toxicity. Given how extensive it was, we made a brutal decision: we threw away about 90% of everything we owned, keeping only what was truly cleanable — metal and glass. Everything porous went.

Then, in January 2021, we did the other radical thing our recovery needed: we flew to Ecuador and rented a place just off the beach in a small fishing village, specifically so we could be somewhere hot — to exercise, sweat, and take a lot of binders, every single day. We stayed for six months. That's not a metaphor for healing; that's literally what mold detox asked of us.

Please read this first: I'm a patient advocate and educator, not a doctor. This is my lived experience and general information — not medical advice, a protocol, or a substitute for professional care. Mold illness is complex, detox can make you feel worse if it's done wrong, and binders and prescription treatments must be guided by a knowledgeable practitioner. Please work with one.
You cannot out-detox a building you're still living in.

Quick answer

How do you detox from mold?

The single most important step is removing yourself from ongoing mold exposure — you cannot detox faster than a water-damaged building can re-expose you. From there, commonly used approaches include binders (such as activated charcoal, bentonite clay, chlorella, or prescription cholestyramine) taken away from food and medications to trap mycotoxins for elimination; supporting the body's drainage pathways (bowels, liver, kidneys, lymph); and sweating through sauna or exercise to help mobilize toxins. This should be guided by a knowledgeable practitioner, because mobilizing toxins without open drainage can make you feel worse.

Key Takeaways

  • Get out of exposure first. No detox works while you’re still breathing the mold — this step is non-negotiable.
  • Mold and Lyme symptoms overlap heavily, and mold can be why “treated” Lyme never resolves.
  • Open your drainage before mobilizing toxins, then use binders as the workhorses; add sweating and heat.
  • We lived it: after test-confirmed toxicity in 2020 we purged ~90% of what we owned and spent six months detoxing on a beach in Ecuador.
  • Detox takes months, not days — and guessing (skipping steps, over-binding) is what keeps people stuck.

What mold toxicity actually feels like

Before the detox steps, it helps to name the thing — because mold toxicity is so often missed for the same reason Lyme is: it doesn't look like one disease. It looks like everything. Mycotoxins are inflammatory, and inflammation touches every system, so the symptom list reads like a shotgun blast. When my family was in it, we had different combinations of the same underlying picture.

The symptoms people report most often include:

  • Bone-deep fatigue — the kind sleep doesn't fix, plus post-exertional crashes.
  • Brain fog & cognitive changes — word-finding trouble, poor short-term memory, difficulty concentrating.
  • Headaches, sinus congestion, and a chronically stuffy or drippy nose that antibiotics never resolve.
  • Air hunger — feeling like you can't get a full, satisfying breath.
  • Mood & nervous-system symptoms — new or worsening anxiety, depression, irritability, and insomnia.
  • Widespread pain — muscle aches, cramps, joint pain, and unusual nerve sensations (tingling, "ice-pick" pains, numbness).
  • Sensory sensitivity — to light, sound, and especially smell (perfume, exhaust, and other buildings suddenly become intolerable).
  • Odd, telltale signs — excessive thirst and frequent urination, temperature dysregulation, static shocks, a metallic taste, red or light-sensitive eyes, night sweats, vertigo, and appetite swings.
  • Gut trouble — bloating, food sensitivities, and reactivity that seems to come out of nowhere.

Notice how much of that overlaps with chronic Lyme, chronic fatigue, fibromyalgia, and anxiety. That overlap is exactly why so many people get a psychiatric label or a shrug instead of a real answer — and why, if this list feels like your life, mold deserves to be on the table.

Why you got sick when others in your house didn't

This is the question that haunts families: we all breathed the same air — why am I the one who's flattened? The answer is largely genetic, and understanding it took a huge weight of self-blame off my shoulders.

Roughly a quarter of people carry HLA-DR immune-gene variants that make it hard to properly "tag" and clear mycotoxins. If your immune system can't flag these toxins for removal, they don't just pass through — they recirculate, and your body stays locked in a chronic inflammatory loop long after the exposure. People without those variants often clear the same toxins and stay relatively fine. Same house, same mold, wildly different outcomes.

When that inflammatory loop takes hold, clinicians often describe it as CIRS — Chronic Inflammatory Response Syndrome, the framework pioneered by Dr. Ritchie Shoemaker. It reframes mold illness as an immune and inflammatory problem, not just "toxins to flush," and it's why a knowledgeable practitioner may track lab markers like C4a, TGF-β1, MMP-9, MSH, and VIP, along with a simple in-office Visual Contrast Sensitivity (VCS) screen. You don't need to memorize the alphabet soup — the point is this: your illness is real, it's measurable, and the reason it hit you hardest isn't weakness. It's biology.

Step 1: Get out of exposure — this is non-negotiable

I'll say it plainly because it's the one thing that isn't optional: you cannot detox faster than a water-damaged building can re-poison you. Every binder, sauna, and supplement is a rounding error if you're still breathing mycotoxins at night. Removing exposure means either professionally remediating the building (done properly, not painted over) or leaving it. For us, the contamination was severe enough that leaving — and eventually relocating our whole detox to Ecuador — was the only real answer.

Before anything else, get honest about your environment. If your home is the source, no protocol will outrun it. Fix the source or get out of it first — everything below assumes you have.

Step 2: The heartbreaking purge (what to keep, what to toss)

Nobody warns you about the grief. When mold contamination is severe, porous items that soak up mycotoxins often can't be fully cleaned — soft furniture, mattresses, most fabrics, paper, cardboard, and similar. Non-porous items — metal, glass, hard sealed surfaces — can usually be cleaned and kept. How far you go depends on the severity and ideally a professional assessment.

In our case, it was extreme: we let go of roughly 90% of our belongings, keeping essentially what was metal or glass — 100% cleanable. It was devastating to do and, honestly, freeing on the other side. I don't say this to tell you to gut your house; I say it so that if you're facing that choice, you know you're not crazy, and you're not alone.

Step 3: Open your drainage before you mobilize toxins

This is the mistake that makes people feel poisoned by their own detox. If you start pulling mycotoxins out of tissues but your exit routes are clogged, they just recirculate and you crash. So the order matters: get your drainage pathways open and moving first — daily bowel movements above all, plus hydration, liver and kidney support, and lymph movement. Some people use gentle tools like enemas or coffee enemas to support elimination; the principle is simply that things have to be able to get out.

Step 4: Binders — the workhorses of mold detox

Binders were the daily backbone of our routine. They're substances that grab onto mycotoxins in your gut so you eliminate them instead of reabsorbing them. Common ones include:

  • Activated charcoal and bentonite clay — widely used general binders.
  • Chlorella and certain fibers — gentler options some people tolerate better.
  • Cholestyramine (CSM) — a prescription binder used specifically in some mold-illness protocols.
The rules that make binders work (and keep them safe): take them away from food, supplements, and medications (often by an hour or two) so they don't bind those instead; drink plenty of water; and keep your bowels moving so the bound toxins actually leave. Dosing and choice — especially prescription binders — belong with your practitioner.

Step 5: Sweat & heat

Here's where Ecuador comes in. Sweating — through exercise, sauna, or a genuinely hot climate — is a well-worn part of many mold-detox approaches, because it helps the body mobilize and release toxins through the skin. We chose to spend six months somewhere hot precisely so we could move, sweat, and take binders consistently, day after day, without fighting a cold environment.

Two honest caveats: sweating works alongside binders and open drainage, not instead of them — you want the mobilized toxins caught and eliminated, not just stirred up. And start gently. Detox reactions are common; more heat faster is not better. Hydrate and replace minerals.

Move it, bind it, get it out — in that order.

Confirming it: mold testing

We didn't guess — testing confirmed our toxicity, and that mattered, both for conviction and for tracking progress. On the body side, practitioners often use urine mycotoxin testing and related markers. On the building side, home tests like an ERMI (or a qualified inspector) help identify whether your environment is the source. Testing turns "maybe it's mold" into a decision you can actually act on — which is exactly what let us commit to something as drastic as we did.

If you suspect mold is also keeping your Lyme recovery stuck, that overlap is real and worth reading about — mold and Lyme feed each other viciously, and many people have to address both. In practice, mold-driven inflammation can keep your immune system too dysregulated to finish clearing a tick-borne infection, and a hidden infection can keep you inflamed enough that mold detox stalls. If you've treated one and stayed sick, look hard at the other.

How long does mold detox take?

I wish I could hand you a tidy number. The truth: it depends on how bad the exposure was, how long it lasted, your genetics, and — above all — how completely you got away from the source. Some people turn a corner in a few months; others need much longer. Our dedicated push was about six months, and healing kept going after that.

The mindset that got us through: mold detox is not a supplement you take — it's an environment you change and a rhythm you keep. Remove exposure, keep drainage open, bind daily, sweat consistently, and be patient with a body that's been through a lot.

The mistakes that keep people stuck

Almost everyone who stalls on mold recovery is making one of a handful of avoidable mistakes. I made a few of them myself before I understood the order of operations:

  1. Detoxing while still in exposure. The big one. You cannot supplement your way out of a building that's still re-dosing you every night. Source first, always.
  2. Mobilizing toxins before drainage is open. Binders and sauna without daily bowel movements and hydration just recirculate what you stirred up — and you feel poisoned by your own protocol.
  3. Going too hard, too fast. Aggressive detox feels productive and backfires. Gentle and consistent beats heroic and bedridden.
  4. Skipping testing. Guessing leaves you unsure and easy to talk out of it. Confirming exposure (body and building) gives you the conviction to make hard changes.
  5. Treating only mold, or only Lyme. When both are present, addressing one and ignoring the other is why so many people plateau.
  6. Quitting too early. Mold recovery isn't linear — you'll have good stretches and dips. The dips aren't failure; they're part of it.
